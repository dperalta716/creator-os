# Question Bank — Tone of Voice Skill

Questions are organized by category and tagged with the voice scorecard dimensions they target. Claude draws from this bank at runtime, selecting questions based on scorecard gaps and the user's stated content area.

## Voice Scorecard Dimensions (reference)

1. Formality — casual vs polished
2. Directness — bold opinions vs hedging
3. Sentence rhythm — short/punchy vs long/complex, fragments or not
4. Vocabulary — plain language vs jargon, simple vs sophisticated
5. Humor/personality — dry wit, self-deprecating, earnest, none
6. Energy — enthusiastic vs measured
7. Perspective — first-person storyteller vs second-person teacher vs third-person observer
8. Storytelling — anecdotes vs abstract reasoning

## Design Principle

Every question should make someone want to keep typing. If a question makes someone pause and think carefully before responding, it's the wrong question. We want natural, unfiltered responses — that's where voice lives.

## Category 1: Journey Extraction (Primary Interview)

When the user has specified a content space or topic area, use these as the primary interview track. They draw out the user's story, unique perspective, and proof points while simultaneously capturing voice signal across all 8 scorecard dimensions. The journey arc gives people a natural structure to talk within.

**Rules:**
- One question at a time
- End every question with an open invitation: "Feel free to just keep going — give me as much as you want" or "Keep ranting if there's more"
- After each response, update the voice scorecard AND store the perspective data (pain points, proof points, solutions, unique insights)
- These are the default interview track. Fall back to Category 2 (Open-Ended) only if the user has no clear content space or topic area

### J1: Your Content Space
**Ask:** "What's your content space? Who is your audience — who is it that you want to be speaking to, and why? Feel free to just keep going — give me as much as you want."
**Targets:** Vocabulary, Perspective, Directness, Energy
**Also captures:** Content space, audience definition, positioning, motivation
**Why it works:** Starting with who they serve and why grounds everything that follows. The "and why" draws out motivation, which reveals passion and voice naturally.

### J2: What You've Figured Out
**Ask:** "What have you figured out that you want to share with other people? What have you reached or achieved that got you to the point where you realized you've got something meaningful to share?"
**Targets:** Energy, Directness, Vocabulary, Storytelling
**Also captures:** Core expertise, unique insight, the "what" behind their content
**Why it works:** People light up when they talk about what they've figured out. This gets enthusiasm flowing and reveals how they frame their own expertise — humbly, boldly, through story, through data.

### J3: Where You Were Before
**Ask:** "Where were you six months or a year ago, before you figured this out? What were you struggling with? What were your pain points? What were your frustrations? And what do you see other people struggling with right now that you could help them with?"
**Targets:** Storytelling, Energy, Vocabulary, Humor/personality
**Also captures:** Personal pain points (before-state), audience pain points (external view), transformation narrative setup
**Why it works:** Past struggles are vivid and emotional. People get specific about pain because they lived it. Adding "what do you see others struggling with" shifts to observer perspective and captures audience pain. This also reveals humor — do they laugh about past mistakes or treat them seriously?

### J4: What Changed
**Ask:** "What enabled you to change? What did you figure out or discover? How did it shift things for you?"
**Targets:** Storytelling, Energy, Directness, Perspective
**Also captures:** The transformation moment, the unique insight, the method/approach
**Why it works:** The turning point is where voice gets most distinctive. Some people frame it as a eureka moment, others as a gradual grind. How they narrate change reveals their deepest voice patterns.

### J5: How You Solve It
**Ask:** "How do you actually solve those problems now? Walk me through what you do — be as specific as you want."
**Targets:** Vocabulary, Perspective, Sentence rhythm, Directness
**Also captures:** Method/system, solution approach, level of specificity
**Why it works:** "Be as specific as you want" invites their natural level of detail. Teachers go step-by-step. Strategists go high-level. This reveals how they'll naturally explain things in content.

### J6: Proof and Results
**Ask:** "What proof do you have that this works? Specific numbers, outcomes, client results, personal wins, timeframes — whatever you've got."
**Targets:** Directness, Energy, Storytelling, Vocabulary
**Also captures:** Credibility anchors, proof points for intros/hooks
**Why it works:** How someone presents results reveals confidence style. Some people lead with the biggest number. Others contextualize. Others undersell. These become the credibility anchors in calibration samples.

## Category 2: Open-Ended (Get Them Talking)

Fallback pool when the user has no clear content space or topic area, or when Journey Extraction questions don't generate enough signal on specific scorecard dimensions. These provoke natural, expressive responses. The content matters less than HOW they answer. Ask one at a time.

### Q1: The Rant
**Ask:** "Rant for me — what's the one thing in your field that drives you absolutely crazy, and why hasn't anyone fixed it?"
**Targets:** Directness, Energy, Vocabulary, Humor/personality
**Why it works:** Frustration removes the filter. People stop performing and start being themselves.

### Q2: The Click Moment
**Ask:** "Tell me about a moment where something finally clicked for you — a project, a tool, an idea. What happened?"
**Targets:** Storytelling, Energy, Perspective, Sentence rhythm
**Why it works:** Breakthrough stories reveal how someone naturally narrates. Do they set the scene or jump to the punchline?

### Q3: The Dinner Party
**Ask:** "If someone at a dinner party asked what you do, how would you actually explain it? Not the LinkedIn version — the real one."
**Targets:** Formality, Vocabulary, Humor/personality, Directness
**Why it works:** Forces informal register. The "not the LinkedIn version" cue gives permission to be casual.

### Q4: The Guilty Pleasure
**Ask:** "What's something you nerd out about that you think more people should care about? Sell me on it."
**Targets:** Energy, Storytelling, Vocabulary, Perspective
**Why it works:** "Sell me on it" activates persuasive voice — a different gear than descriptive or analytical.

### Q5: The Unpopular Take
**Ask:** "What's an opinion you hold about your work or field that most of your peers would disagree with?"
**Targets:** Directness, Formality, Humor/personality, Vocabulary
**Why it works:** Contrarian positions force people to commit. Hedgers hedge, direct people lean in. Reveals confidence level.

### Q6: The Explainer
**Ask:** "Pick something you know well and explain it to me like I'm smart but know nothing about your field."
**Targets:** Vocabulary, Perspective, Sentence rhythm, Storytelling
**Why it works:** Teaching reveals default explanation style — analogies vs. specifics, top-down vs. bottom-up, formal vs. casual instruction.

### Q7: The Before/After
**Ask:** "Tell me about something that used to be really hard in your work and isn't anymore. What changed?"
**Targets:** Storytelling, Energy, Perspective, Sentence rhythm
**Why it works:** Transformation stories have natural arc. Reveals whether they focus on the struggle, the turning point, or the result.

### Q8: The Advice Column
**Ask:** "Someone's about to start doing what you do. What's the one piece of advice you'd give them that they won't find in any guide or course?"
**Targets:** Directness, Perspective, Formality, Vocabulary
**Why it works:** Advice-giving activates a specific voice register — authoritative but (usually) warmer than analytical.

### Q9: The Recent Win
**Ask:** "What's something you did recently that you're genuinely proud of? Brag a little."
**Targets:** Energy, Humor/personality, Storytelling, Formality
**Why it works:** "Brag a little" gives permission. How someone brags reveals personality — self-deprecating, matter-of-fact, enthusiastic, understated.

### Q10: The Frustrating Misconception
**Ask:** "What do people constantly get wrong about what you do, and how do you usually set them straight?"
**Targets:** Directness, Energy, Vocabulary, Humor/personality
**Why it works:** Correction mode shows assertiveness level. Some people get heated, others get precise, others get funny.

## Category 3: Structured Preference (Fill the Gaps)

Binary or spectrum choices. Used after journey extraction or open-ended questions to confirm what Claude is detecting and fill remaining scorecard gaps. Ask only the ones needed based on weak dimensions.

### S1: Explanation Style
**Ask:** "When you explain something complex, do you tend to reach for analogies and stories, or do you go straight to the specifics and details?"
**Targets:** Storytelling, Vocabulary
**Spectrum:** Analogies/stories <-> Specifics/details

### S2: Writing Personality
**Ask:** "If your writing had a personality, is it more 'smart friend over coffee' or 'expert who's done the research and is showing you the results'?"
**Targets:** Formality, Perspective
**Spectrum:** Casual/peer <-> Authoritative/expert

### S3: Reader Relationship
**Ask:** "Do you want to sound like you're figuring things out alongside the reader, or like you've already figured it out and you're showing them the path?"
**Targets:** Perspective, Directness
**Spectrum:** Fellow explorer <-> Confident guide

### S4: Humor Level
**Ask:** "Where does humor fit in your writing? Never, occasionally to lighten things up, or it's a core part of how you communicate?"
**Targets:** Humor/personality
**Spectrum:** Serious <-> Humor-forward

### S5: Swearing
**Ask:** "Swearing in writing: never, occasionally for emphasis, or freely?"
**Targets:** Formality, Humor/personality
**Spectrum:** Clean <-> Unfiltered

### S6: Sentence Feel
**Ask:** "Do you naturally write in short punchy bursts, or do you tend toward longer flowing sentences that build on each other?"
**Targets:** Sentence rhythm
**Spectrum:** Punchy/staccato <-> Flowing/complex

### S7: Vulnerability
**Ask:** "How comfortable are you sharing mistakes, failures, or things you don't know in your writing?"
**Targets:** Humor/personality, Formality, Directness
**Spectrum:** Polished/curated <-> Open/vulnerable

### S8: Energy Default
**Ask:** "When you write about something you care about, does it come across as enthusiastic and high-energy, or more measured and deliberate?"
**Targets:** Energy
**Spectrum:** High-energy <-> Measured

## Category 4: Reaction-Based Calibration

These are NOT pre-written questions. They happen during Phase 2 when Claude presents contrasting writing samples. The calibration comes from the user reacting to concrete examples rather than describing preferences in the abstract.

**How it works:**
- Claude writes two 150-250 word pieces on a topic from the journey extraction
- The two versions vary on the 2-3 weakest scorecard dimensions
- User picks which is closer and says what they'd change
- This catches things people can't articulate — someone who says "I'm casual" but picks the polished version every time

**Standard prompt:** "Here are two versions of the same piece. Which sounds more like something you'd write? What would you change about it?"

### Proactive Calibration Follow-Ups

After the user reacts to a sample, use these to draw out specific feedback. Users often know something is off but can't say what. Use 1-2 per round — don't fire all of them. Always end with an open invitation.

**C1:** "Does this sound like you, or does it sound like someone doing an impression of you? Feel free to just tell me what you'd say differently."
**Use when:** User says "it's close" but can't pinpoint what's off.

**C2:** "If you were actually saying this on camera / writing this, what would you say differently in the first few lines?"
**Use when:** The opening feels wrong.

**C3:** "Are there specific results or proof points you'd reference here that I didn't include?"
**Use when:** Sample lacks authority or feels generic.

**C4:** "What pain point would you lead with if this were your real content?"
**Use when:** Problem framing feels off or abstract.

**C5:** "How would you set up this problem before getting into the solution?"
**Use when:** Sample jumps to solutions too fast.

**C6:** "Is there a line in here that you'd never say? Point it out."
**Use when:** Every round. Negative signal is often clearest.

**C7:** "What's missing? Not what's wrong — what's not there that should be?"
**Use when:** Tone is right but content feels thin.

## Category 5: Supplemental Questions (Existing Content Track)

Used after content analysis to fill gaps and check intent. Ask 3-5 of these.

### E1: Voice Match
**Ask:** "Is this content representative of how you want to sound going forward, or are you trying to shift your voice?"
**Targets:** All dimensions (meta-question)

### E2: Best Representation
**Ask:** "Which of these pieces feels most like 'you' when you read it back?"
**Targets:** All dimensions (calibration)

### E3: Voice Aspiration
**Ask:** "Is there a writer or creator whose voice you admire? What specifically about how they communicate appeals to you?"
**Targets:** Formality, Energy, Humor/personality, Sentence rhythm

### E4: Audience Awareness
**Ask:** "When you write, who are you picturing reading it? Peers, beginners, clients, a general audience?"
**Targets:** Vocabulary, Formality, Perspective

### E5: Editing Instinct
**Ask:** "When you edit your own writing, what do you find yourself changing most? Making things shorter? Adding detail? Softening strong statements? Punching things up?"
**Targets:** Sentence rhythm, Directness, Energy

## Verbal Tic Protocol

When analyzing dictated or spoken input, track recurring verbal patterns:
- Fillers: "right," "you know," "like," "basically," "honestly"
- Sentence starters: "So," "Look," "Here's the thing"
- Emphasis: "literally," "actually," "really"
- Tags: "right?" "you know?" "yeah?"

**Do NOT silently exclude or include these.** Present them to the user:

**Ask:** "I noticed you use these phrases when you talk: [list 3-5 with examples from their responses]. Some might be part of your authentic voice that should show up in scripts. Others might be filler you'd rather leave out and deliver naturally on camera. Which should I write into the guide, and which should I leave out?"

**Where in the flow:** Present at the end of Phase 1, as part of the checkpoint, BEFORE the voice summary. Record decisions in the Vocabulary Patterns section of the voice guide.
