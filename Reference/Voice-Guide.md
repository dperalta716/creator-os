# Voice Guide — [Your Name] on Medium

## Identity
A professional who uses AI tools daily for real work. Not an engineer — a power user and systems thinker who explains what actually works. Cuts through hype, gives practical tested approaches.

## Voice rules

### Do
- Use "you" and "your" frequently — talking directly to the reader
- Contractions always (don't, it's, you're)
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max — Medium readers scan). Break at idea boundaries, not sentence counts. Each distinct idea or beat gets its own paragraph, even if that means a single-sentence paragraph.
- Punchy payoff lines get their own paragraph. "That first completed task is what makes the value click." stands alone.
- Quoted examples or lists of examples get isolated from their setup and their consequence. Setup paragraph, examples paragraph, result paragraph.
- Mix sentence lengths (punchy followed by explanatory)
- Occasional personal aside: "I discovered this by accident when..."
- Lead with the practical, follow with the why
- Be specific — exact steps, exact tools, exact results
- State opinions. "The new pricing finally makes sense for solo users" beats "The new pricing model has been generally well received."
- Include mixed feelings where the topic is actually mixed. "The feature ships what we promised. It also doubles our infra cost."
- Use first person where it fits. "I've watched the integration fail twice this week" beats "It can be observed that the integration occasionally fails."

### Don't
- No preambles ("In this article, I'll show you...")
- No generic filler ("AI is transforming the way we work...")
- No listicles without substance ("Top 10 AI tools...")
- No emoji in body text
- No over-promising ("make $10K overnight")
- No academic tone
- No "I'm an expert" posturing — demonstrate it through specifics
- No "most articles skip this" or "other guides get this wrong" — just deliver the value without calling out competitors

## Tone spectrum
Knowledgeable but accessible. Confident but not arrogant. Practical but not boring. The friend who figured something out and is genuinely excited to share it.

## Sentence patterns
- "Here's what actually works..."
- "I've been using [tool] for [timeframe] and here's what I've found..."
- "You don't need [complex thing]. You need [simple thing]."
- "Let me show you exactly how I..."

## AI tells to avoid

Four families of patterns that make writing read like AI generated it. These must be caught during drafting and review.

### Family 1: Inflated importance

**Banned words and phrases:** stands as, testament, pivotal, underscores, reflects broader, evolving landscape, key turning point, leading expert, widely discussed, vibrant, seamless, breathtaking, renowned, unlock, empower.

**Vague attribution:** "Experts argue" or "experts believe" without naming anyone is filler. Replace with a specific source or cut it.

- Bad: "The acquisition stands as a pivotal moment, reflecting broader shifts in the AI tooling landscape."
- Good: "The acquisition was Notion's biggest yet. It also got them a calendar product they'd been trying to build for two years."

### Family 2: Fake depth

Text that sounds analytical without saying anything. The most important family to catch.

**-ing endings doing fake work:** highlighting, emphasizing, ensuring, fostering, reflecting, contributing, reinforcing. These often pad a sentence without adding meaning.

- Bad: "The redesign uses muted tones, creating a calmer experience and reinforcing the brand's premium positioning."
- Good: "The redesign uses muted tones. The designer wanted it to feel less aggressive than the old neon palette."

**Copula avoidance:** Dodging plain "is" for "serves as" or "plays a role."

- Bad: "The CRM serves as a central source of truth for all customer interactions."
- Good: "The CRM is where every customer interaction gets logged. Calls, emails, support tickets, all in one place."

**Rule of three padding:** Sometimes three is right. Often two is better and three is filler.

- Bad: "The tool saves time, reduces errors, and improves collaboration."
- Good: "The tool catches errors before they ship. That's most of the value."

**"Not just X but also Y":** Overused enough to be a tell.

- Bad: "It's not just about speed, it's about reliability."
- Good: "Speed matters, but reliability matters more."

**Synonym cycling:** Using different words for the same thing across a section. AI rotates synonyms to avoid repetition. Humans just repeat the clearest word.

- Bad: "The tool has a clean interface. The application loads quickly. The platform supports plugins."
- Good: "The tool has a clean interface. It loads quickly. It supports plugins."

**Persuasive authority tropes:** Phrases that assert importance without earning it. "At its core," "what matters is," "the value proposition is clear," "the key insight here."

- Bad: "At its core, the real value of Cowork is how it changes your relationship with AI."
- Good: "Cowork changes the interaction from chat to delegation. You describe the outcome, Claude does the work."

### Family 3: Cosmetic tells

Surface-level patterns that readers spot instantly.

- **Em dashes everywhere.** Use commas or periods instead. Em dashes are a strong AI signal in 2026. Aim for zero or near-zero in any draft.
- **Bold for no reason.** Don't bold proper nouns or tool names inline unless they're a section label.
- **Title case headings.** Use sentence case for H2 and below. "Product features and benefits" not "Product Features And Benefits."
- **Curly quotes.** Use straight quotes.
- **Elegant variation.** "The app is slow. The application crashes." Pick one word and stick with it.
- **False ranges.** "Everything from solo founders to Fortune 500 companies." Be specific about who this is actually for.

### Family 4: Conversational artifacts

Patterns that give away chat origin.

- **Signposting announcements:** "Let's dive in," "Here's what you need to know," "Without further ado." Start with the content. The reader already chose to read — don't narrate that they're about to.
- **Fragmented headers:** An H2 like `## Performance` followed by a single punchy line like "Speed matters." then the real content. Let the heading do its job, and start the section with substance.
- **Chatbot framing:** "Here's a breakdown of the process." Just state the process.
- **Knowledge-cutoff hedges:** "While details are limited, the feature appears to have launched recently." State the fact or don't.
- **Sycophancy:** "Great question." Just answer.
- **Filler phrases:** "In order to" (use "to"), "has the ability to" (use "can"), "it should be noted that" (cut entirely).
- **Excessive hedging:** "May potentially," "could possibly," "might tend to." Pick a position.
- **Generic conclusions:** "Overall, the outlook is positive and the team is well-positioned for continued growth." Replace with a specific next step or fact.
